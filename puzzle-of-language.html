Puzzle of Language — An Azerbaijani, English, Turkish, French poem

I — səni əclaf — your — dunu satdın
I — dəli sərxoş — sour — gulu yandın
Am — ziyi — am — din — dinlə tapdın
Artır — I — qız — man — i — sona aldır

Sour — ma — ay — kız kendini aldır
Dur — ma — çay — ax sırtını kaldır
Your man hanged me — dalgalandır
Sour man softened — şaxtalandır

Voices, noises, which one exist?
How far we are? La langue — seçdir
Sometimes the — ma
Sometimes the — ay
Sometimes — əmziy
What time softened?
— Babak Jabbar Nezhad

Glossary

əmziy — in South Azerbaijan dialect menas pacifier.

Critique and Commentary

This poem cannot be read passively. It must be entered as a linguistic event. The reader’s mind becomes the stage where four languages — Azerbaijani, English, Turkish, and French — fracture and mirror one another. Phonetics destabilize grammar. Emotional codes are encoded in syllables. This is not a poem of clarity, but of collision — a confrontation between the languages that built the poet’s identity and the shadows they cast.

The first stanza introduces disrupted syntax and mixed phonetic tones. A line like “I — dəli sərxoş — sour — gulu yandın” is a hybrid of emotional invocation and fragmented translation — *I — mad drunk — sour — you burned the rose.* But each phrase overlaps with emotional metaphor and phonetic dissonance, like broken whispers from overlapping tongues.

The second stanza becomes more chaotic. The visual rhymes (ma/ay/kız), shifts in tempo (“dalgalandır”, “şaxtalandır”), and the auditory ambiguity force the reader to abandon normal semantic search and **listen instead to the architecture of sound**. There is violence and softening — “Your man hanged me / sour man softened” — suggesting linguistic trauma that both harms and heals.

Then comes the existential rupture: “Voices, noises, which one exist?” A question not only of meaning, but of **ontological structure**. Does language construct or dissolve self? And is there a “self” beneath its shifting frames?

The final lines (“Sometimes the — ma / Sometimes the — ay”) resist completion. They are rhythm gates. They **interrupt the reader’s inner voice**, destabilize syntactic expectation, and create **sonic loops** that echo across multiple languages. Each syllable becomes a cipher for emotional fields not yet categorized.

Extended Commentary

The poem contains phonetic shadows — echoes across suppressed languages. Certain consonant clusters recall forgotten pronunciations, like a voice remembering what was never taught. You don’t just read this — it reads you back. Even your breath has to adapt.

Lines like “Sometimes the — ma” are not mistakes or gaps; they are **codes** — emotional signals embedded in sound. To read them aloud is to become an antenna for meaning across languages that don’t trust each other.

Consonant and vowel clusters work like **symbolic pulses**. “əmziy” doesn’t mean something; it hums something. Like a cracked lullaby that only makes sense in sleep. Babak uses language the way early mystics used script — not for truth, but to awaken the parts of the brain that logic can’t reach.
