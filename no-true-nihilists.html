No True Nihilists: A Critique of Hidden Longing in Khayyam, Hedayat, Kafka, and Camus

Western and Persian thinkers alike have often spoken of nihilism, absurdity, and existential crisis—but rarely with full honesty. In this essay, I argue that none of the four figures most often associated with existential or nihilistic thought—Omar Khayyam, Sadegh Hedayat, Franz Kafka, and Albert Camus—ever truly severed themselves from the desire for God. And by "God," I do not mean a religious entity, but something deeper: the desire for continuity, fairness, love, and the preservation of life itself.

Hedayat and Khayyam: Cloaked Mystics

Sadegh Hedayat, in The Blind Owl, is often read as a nihilist, a man broken by meaninglessness. But his writing is flooded with symbolic longing. Not only does he borrow from the form of Sufism—the beloved, the dual self, the opium of detachment—but he also submits to its emotional structure. His narrator mourns. He aches. He turns to decay only after love has failed. For Hedayat, God is not absent; rather, God is everything he lost: love, justice, tradition, safety, belonging.

Omar Khayyam goes further in rejecting spiritual form. He speaks with clarity: “There is no answer—drink the wine.” His voice is often seen as nihilistic, yet even here, there is a residual desire for fairness and continuation. His protest against fate is a cry for a world that should have been just. Like Hedayat, he does not kill the gods—he only complains that they are silent. His is a clearer but still emotionally dependent position.

Kafka and Camus: The Abstract Cry

Kafka, especially in The Metamorphosis, is more abstract in form. His structure is dreamlike, unreal, and disconnected from religious or cultural scaffolding. But his emotional content still mourns the sacred. In the scene where the dying insect is suffering alone while, in the next room, the family discusses marriage and the red-cheeked daughter, Kafka expresses a deep longing for fairness and life’s continuity. He doesn’t philosophize about God—but he suffers as if God, or love, or justice, were supposed to exist. This is not pure nihilism. It is the quiet pain of a man who never received what he was told life contained.

Camus, in The Stranger, achieves a colder, more abstract voice. His protagonist is detached from morality, numb to social emotions, and indifferent even to death. But Camus's abstraction forgets one thing: love is not optional for the human being. He assumes that a person can become fully numb—but fails to acknowledge that numbness itself often grows from a past where love was unmet. A child cries from fear, not from rationality. And it is this fear that creates the need for love. Camus may reject love as meaningless, but he cannot erase its biological root.

The Deeper Structure: Fear Before Love

This essay rests on a fundamental idea:

Love emerges from fear.

When we are born, we do not cry for beauty or meaning—we cry out of terror. We meet the world in need. And from the response to that need—milk, warmth, protection—emerges what we later call love. Love is not born in transcendence; it is born in survival. And survival is born in fear.

Thus, to speak of a human being who does not love is to speak of a being that never feared. But this is biologically impossible. Even those who mask it in logic, detachment, or coldness—like Camus’s Meursault—have it buried inside, shaped by disappointment or trauma, hidden beneath complexes or super-egoic defense.

Conclusion: No True Nihilists

None of these writers are true nihilists. Not Khayyam, who laughs at fate but still craves fairness. Not Hedayat, who cloaks his despair in mystic longing. Not Kafka, who mourns quietly for justice. Not Camus, who detaches from love, but forgets that love grows from fear—and fear is part of every life.

The only honest path is this:

To admit that we want what may not exist.
To name the ache, not pretend it is gone.
To see that even abstraction can be emotional.

They were not liars. But they were not free. And maybe none of us are. Not yet.

— Babak Jabbar Nezhad
Daş Maku, West Azerbaijan
