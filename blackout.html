When Light Fails: Testimony from a Broken Grid

1. A Lamp Above Our Heads

We do not fear the dark.
We fear what it brings.

In a small room in Maku, a family sits beneath a broken lamp — cracked, heavy, glowing like a threat. It looks like a monster, ready to explode. Inside the room, a little girl runs to her father’s arms, trembling. The father holds her. The lamp may burst. Everyone watches — mother, son, daughter, father — not as a family, but as witnesses waiting for judgment.

This is not metaphor. This is their ceiling.

2. Six Hours Without Breath

Electricity is not just about light. It is about air, breath, safety, and time.
In Maku, the government’s “love” means six hours of blackout each day — triple the national average. There is no schedule. The man in the electricity office cuts power as he wishes.

When the electricity dies:

  • Elevators stop between floors
  • Oxygen machines fail
  • Card readers in shops go silent
  • ATMs spit out two dollars — barely enough for bread
  • Refrigerators melt
  • Clinics close
  • And lives tremble

My aunt’s 80-year-old husband — who can barely walk — got trapped between floors. When they pulled him out, his arm was bleeding. The next day, he put on a clean shirt. My aunt said, “Be careful — it’s bloody.”
He answered, “It’s okay.”
Because here, this is okay. This is how we live.

3. Bumps, Stairs, and Silent Screams

What is abnormal in other countries is normal in Iran.

In every 100 meters of Maku’s streets, there is a giant speed bump — huge, irregular, aggressive. These are not tools for safety. They are weapons of laziness. Instead of officers controlling cars, they installed pain on the road.

I’ve felt that pain.
When we took my mother — post-knee surgery — to Tabriz in an ambulance, every bump sent shockwaves through her healing body. She groaned. We held her. But the road held nothing back.

Medical buildings are no better. Most have stairs. Orthopedic doctors — whose patients cannot walk — rent offices on the second floor. Radiologists — who serve the injured — work in basements. I once carried my mother, in a wheelchair, down those stairs to reach radiography. Three men helped. It was not an emergency. It was routine.

I once took my father, stiff with Parkinson’s, to a neurologist in Urmia. He could barely walk. But the doctor’s office stood above him, at the top of a staircase.

Why? Because doctors in Iran are no longer healers. They are businessmen. They choose where rent is cheap, not where patients can reach.

4. The Meaning of Normal

So when my aunt’s husband shrugs off the blood on his shirt, it is not apathy. It is survival.

He knows the system is broken — and he has lived long enough to stop resisting.
In Iran, the abnormal has become ordinary.

Suffering is not an event. It is a pattern. A rhythm. A heartbeat.
We don’t file lawsuits. We don’t demand better elevators. We adapt. We improvise.
We memorize ATM hours. We ride shock-bumps in ambulances.
We fear light because it might explode.
We carry wheelchairs down staircases built by the uncaring.

And through it all — we still dress our wounds, and say:
“It’s okay.”

But it is not okay.
It only feels like it.

Written by Babak Jabbar Nezhad
Daş Maku, West Azerbaijan
